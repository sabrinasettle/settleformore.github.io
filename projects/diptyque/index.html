Redesigning Diptyque

July 2023 to September 2023

Overview

Redesigning Diptyque

Revitalizing the Diptyque website: a creative project for including more character and usability.

Problem

A well known and established brand is indistinguishable from competitors and could use a an update to be more aligned with their history and ethos. By ignoring that in their digital identity they are reducing the enjoyment of their products and have less of a creative edge over competitors in their online shopping experience.

Goals

Redesign a responsive platform with references to the brands history and identity with improved clarity for faster conversion.

Tools

  • Figma
  • Procreate
  • Adobe Suite
  • Freeform

Understanding

User Research

Identifying current pain points and what users want and need

3 informal interviews were conducted with individuals who are interested in buying scented candles and perfumes. Users were asked a set of questions and we went over the site and how it was set up to either be misaligned or aligned with their discovery needs as well as the final conversion through completing a purchase. Primary pain points that came up were that the users felt the overall phrasing was unhelpful and at times redundant, a confusing navigation that made it difficult to find items, while also not having enough information about the scents to confidently make a online purchase.

  • What do they mean by colored candles?
  • The candle classification doesn't make sense.
  • It doesn't make sense to do it by size first because you're looking for a scent, not a size.Generally.
  • There should be more filtering
  • It doesn't seem very consistently designed.
  • I dont understand the sort order.
  • Yeah, they were trying to do a creative twist on things.
  • I can't really find things by scent families.

User Feedback

Synthesized perceptions and perspectives

All users had issues with the main navigation, citing wording and page hierarchy being misleading or time consuming

While using the sort and filter features users faced confusing language and terms that aren't clear, resulting in the inability to quickly sort or filter

Most users found that design of site to be equivalent to the pricing of the products, but found it to be lacking in establishing the brand's history and unique identity.

Market and Company Research

Competitive Analysis

Understanding market offerings and scent language

I analyzed 4 direct competitors to gain understanding on how information on scents was presented. In doing so was able understand the basics of olfactory language for selling scented candles and perfumes as well as the world building of scent based shopping experiences.

Brand History

Diptyque was founded by Yves Coueslant, Christiane Gautrot, and Desmond Knox-Leet, three curious friends who were artists, designers, interior decorators and art lovers, in 1961 selling fabric and handmade crafts like colorful candles. Their iconic oval labels and product shaping was inspired by the elongated shield of a Roman Praetorian Guard solider, originally introduced in a fabric by the three founders. SInce the founding its now a recognizable and global brand with its handmade candles and classic personal scents for room and body.

Olfactory terms in Navigation

While reviewing competitors I also took note of the language and organization of their header navigation for new designs of the Diptyque navigation to better accommodate user's needs.

Design Process

Taking Inspiration

Historical art as a inspiration for modern interfaces

The founders of Diptyque were inspired by the ancient world. I similarly took inspiration from roman steles and medieval diptychs. I decided to use the diptychs two sided styling as inspiration on how to divide the layout and where the drawers would open to mimic a equal divide.

Sketching

Sketches, flows, and ideas

A main focus and iteration point in the sketching phase was the exploration pages for candles and how the information was presented. I wanted the site to offer an sophisticated experience while drawing on the history and identity of the brand, as well as interesting interactions for a faster exploration of olfactory families (floral, woody, fruits, etc.) and the brands well known and much loved scent collection, a group of items with the same scent.

Wireframes

Ideating and early stages of design

Low-fidelty wireframes were created after rounds of crazy eights, sketching, and card sorting for link structure.

Usability Testing

Study Details

Using a small group of three participants I tested with them the mid fidelity prototype. The focus of the study was to determine if there was an improvement from the company's website in text tone and access to products. All the participants agreed that there was a significant difference and the prototype was easier and more fun to use. From their insights on what wasnt working were visual elements that were off

  • Are changes an improvement and aligned with user needs?

  • Is there any pain points for improvement in the flow?

  • Is the scent speciality language hindering a purchase conversion?

Study Results

After receiving responses I created an affinity map and from that map determined priority of tasks to complete before moving on to the high fidelity mockups and the prototyping.

  • Users found the many connections to the brand's universe to be unnecessary

  • Recommendations were found to useful but users wanted clarity as to why items were being recommended

  • Users did not not have any indications of what was beneath the the foldline of the main page

  • A high priority was to redesign the cart page, samples were hidden from users as well as the design being jarring

Visual Design

Colors and Fonts

Brand identity and and visual language

The previous color way of black and white was harsh and its accessibility improved by choosing softer shades. Inspired by the sketches of the founders of Diptyque I choose neutrals like a navy black ink color and the different shades of paper with white to a warm cream. This change allowed the black and white labeling of the candles and the others to pop.

Diptyque has its own serif font and for this project I ended up choosing the Google Font, Cardo as a substitute, paired with Montserrant as a modern sans serif font to larger texts and smaller navigation.

Final Designs

Candles Page

A place to explore all things candles

The candles page was designed as the gateway to a comprehensive candle experience, serving as a seamless portal to Diptyque's aromatic world. It not only provides easy access to the entire candle collection but also connects users with olfactive families, guiding them through the nuanced artistry of scents. The page integrates 'scent stories,' an immersive storytelling carousel, amplifying the brand's narrative.

Cart Page

Creatively presenting a secure and tidy checkout

The cart reuses creative elements such as buttons without jeopardizing the users need for security, clarity, and honesty. It features easy removal of items and a clear way to add complementary samples into the cart before checking out.

Product Page

A page that celebrates the Diptyque products and their stories

Users had trouble conceiving of a scent through the previous information. I added a scent intensity meter and more text about the scent so that the user may have more information. Also added was a slider with materials.

Scent Collection and Olfactory Family pages

Organizing and categorizing the different scents

Each page serves as a curated odyssey, guiding users through a sensory adventure. These pages help users get more directly to their interests, while allowing the user to wander through and explore.

Challenges and Constraints

In the pursuit of redesigning the Diptyque website, several challenges and constraints came into play, testing the creative boundaries of the project. One major hurdle was to preserve the essence and elegance that Diptyque is renowned for, while infusing fresh and innovative design elements. Balancing this fine line between tradition and modernity is paramount, requiring a deep understanding of the brand's heritage and the discerning tastes of its clientele.

  • Difficulties in designing for the correct terminology and image aspect ratio in the case of cards and the explore pages and sections.

  • How do we make it accessible and formatted in a way that users can relate previous experiences and conceptual models to?

Continuous Improvement and Next Steps

Final Thoughts

In the ever-evolving landscape of digital design, the genesis of this project emerged from a visual phenomenon observed across numerous brandsa trend of 'Re-Blanding,' where the fine line between uniqueness and uniformity became blurred. It raised a fundamental question: How can we elevate creativity as a deliberate design choice without sacrificing the comforting familiarity that users seek within the confines of online design standards? Striking this delicate equilibrium is not merely a matter of aesthetics but a pivotal determinant of user engagement and brand authenticity. The challenge lies not just in pushing the boundaries of creativity but in doing so with a keen understanding of the user's psychological comfort zones. Through this case study, I have embarked on a journey to redefine online design paradigms, ensuring that creativity not only captivates but also resonates, fostering a digital space where innovation and user familiarity harmoniously coexist. The project was one where I learned to push design with inspiration from a brand, to meld it with my own personal preferences and user empathy focused research.

Next Steps

At the end I believe that the design could be pushed further, creating greater alignment with business needs and user needs by:

  • Implementing features that provide pickup or even consultations at boutiques

  • Using analytics to provide more quantitative numbers to determine future iterations and features

  • Another usability study to determine new priorities for new features and current feature refinement

  • A quick add feature on hovering over the product cards
